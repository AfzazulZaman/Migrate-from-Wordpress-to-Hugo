Top 10 Extinct Animals That Came Back: The Wonders of De-Extinction

January 18, 2025

De-extinction, the process of bringing extinct animals back to life, has long been a concept of science fiction. However, with advancements in genetic engineering, cloning, and other scientific innovations, some once-extinct species have been brought back into existence, or at least are close to making a return. Here’s a look at 10 animals that were thought to be lost forever but are making a surprising comeback.

The Pyrenean ibex, a wild goat species native to the Pyrenees Mountains in Europe, was declared extinct in 2000 due to hunting and habitat loss. However, scientists managed to clone a Pyrenean ibex in 2003 using frozen cells from the last known individual. Unfortunately, the cloned ibex died shortly after birth, but the success of the cloning process showed the potential for de-extinction.

One of the most famous extinct animals, the woolly mammoth roamed the Earth during the Ice Age before disappearing about 4,000 years ago. Scientists are working on a project to bring the woolly mammoth back using advanced genetic techniques, including cloning and gene-editing tools like CRISPR. The goal is to create a hybrid that possesses woolly mammoth traits, such as cold resistance and shaggy fur, to help restore the tundra ecosystem.

The thylacine, or Tasmanian tiger, was declared extinct in the 1930s due to hunting and habitat destruction. However, efforts to resurrect this marsupial predator are underway. Using preserved DNA samples, researchers are attempting to clone the thylacine or bring it back through gene-editing techniques. The hope is that one day, the thylacine will again roam the forests of Tasmania.

Once the most numerous bird in North America, the passenger pigeon was driven to extinction in the early 20th century due to overhunting and habitat destruction. Scientists are now working to revive the species by studying the DNA of preserved passenger pigeon specimens. They aim to use advanced techniques, such as cloning and gene-editing, to reintroduce the species to the wild.

The Baiji dolphin, a freshwater species native to the Yangtze River in China, was declared functionally extinct in 2006 due to habitat loss and pollution. However, there have been efforts to bring this dolphin back to life through cloning. Scientists have been able to extract DNA from preserved Baiji specimens and are exploring ways to revive the species using cloning techniques, although no successful births have been recorded yet.

The aurochs was a large, wild ancestor of domestic cattle, roaming across Europe, Asia, and North Africa until its extinction in the 17th century. Efforts to bring back the aurochs involve selective breeding, where modern cattle species are bred to exhibit characteristics similar to those of the extinct aurochs. Though not a direct clone, this method of “back-breeding” aims to recreate the physical traits and behavior of the extinct animal.

The great auk, a flightless seabird, was hunted to extinction in the 19th century. The bird lived along the coasts of the North Atlantic and was known for its distinctive black-and-white plumage. Scientists are working on sequencing the DNA of preserved great auk specimens, and there are ongoing discussions about using advanced technologies to potentially bring the species back to life.

The Pinta Island tortoise, native to the Galápagos Islands, was thought to be extinct after the death of its last known member, Lonesome George, in 2012. However, researchers have been working to find closely related tortoises that could be cross-bred with the Pinta Island tortoise to recreate the species. Efforts continue to locate and breed these tortoises to restore the genetic line of the Pinta Island tortoise.

The sabretooth cat, or Smilodon, roamed the Americas during the Pleistocene era but went extinct around 10,000 years ago. Scientists are attempting to bring the sabretooth back using DNA extracted from preserved fossils. While full de-extinction is not yet possible, researchers are exploring the possibility of recreating a close relative with the same saber-like teeth and predatory behavior.

The mammoth steppe bison, which once roamed the northern regions of North America during the Ice Age, became extinct over 10,000 years ago. In recent years, scientists have been able to extract DNA from preserved specimens and have started to work on bringing this bison species back using cloning and genetic engineering. The goal is to recreate the animals and reintroduce them into their native environment.

The Future of De-Extinction#

The science of de-extinction has made great strides, and although many challenges remain, it opens up fascinating possibilities for biodiversity conservation. While the return of these extinct animals could restore lost ecosystems and provide valuable insights into evolutionary biology, it also raises important ethical questions. The success of these projects depends not only on technology but also on whether ecosystems can sustain the reintroduced species.

De-extinction may one day help restore some of the creatures that have vanished from our world, but it also serves as a stark reminder of the importance of preserving the animals and ecosystems we still have today. As technology continues to evolve, the line between science fiction and reality becomes increasingly blurred, making the possibility of bringing extinct animals back to life an exciting and complex field of study.
